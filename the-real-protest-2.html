A Democracy Doesn't Need Louder Voices, It Needs Better Informed Citizens

By Khushansh B

My Dear Gullible Indians,

I see that many of you genuinely love this country, and it is heartening to see so many young people willing to stand up for a cause they believe in. In a democracy, every citizen has the constitutional right to protest peacefully, and they absolutely should when they believe an injustice has occurred.

Many of my friends and family have been sharing posts to pressure the government over the NEET paper leak. The cause itself is valid. Students spend years preparing for these examinations, and when the integrity of the system is compromised, accountability is necessary. The emotional toll on students has been immense, and every such failure deserves serious attention.

However, if you look at the timeline objectively, many of the corrective steps had already been initiated before the protests gained momentum. If those actions were already underway, then an obvious question arises: at what point did this stop being only about NEET or the students?

Do we really believe that for every failure in this country, the Prime Minister or the Education Minister is personally responsible? Even if one believes ministers should be held accountable, is demanding resignation always the only or the correct solution? These are questions worth asking instead of simply following whatever narrative is trending online.

I believe this is precisely how genuine public anger can sometimes be redirected toward broader political objectives. Large protests often attract different organisations, activists, influencers, and political interests, each with their own agenda. That possibility should not be ignored simply because it is uncomfortable to discuss.

For eighteen days, the protests remained peaceful and no one interfered. However, when sections of the crowd allegedly began damaging public property, attacking vehicles, metro infrastructure, and government buildings, it became the responsibility of the police and security forces to restore order. At the same time, if security personnel committed excesses or used disproportionate force, they too must be investigated and held accountable. Accountability cannot be selective. At the end of the day, all we are seeing is mischievous sections on both sides exploiting this situation while the country as a whole pays the price.

A democracy is only as good as its people. Our politicians are, in many ways, a reflection of us as a society. Blindly supporting or opposing any side without understanding the facts weakens democracy rather than strengthening it.

What concerns me even more is the growing culture of protesting simply because it has become fashionable. Many people join movements without understanding the issue in depth. Influencers jump on every trending topic for visibility. Others show solidarity without ever asking who is actually behind the leak, or what happened after it was discovered.

Because if you actually trace what happened, it wasn't a minister who leaked the paper — it was, by the investigation's own account, external educators hired to conduct the exam, who sold question papers on to a private coaching network, with named individuals accused for specific sections of the exam. Framing this as one man's personal crime is its own kind of misinformation.

And the timeline itself tells a fuller story than most people bother to check. Within days of the complaint surfacing in May, the matter was referred to the CBI, a state investigative agency opened its own probe, an FIR was registered, the exam was cancelled and a re-exam announced — all inside roughly a week. Over the following days, the CBI arrested multiple intermediaries and key accused. The Education Minister publicly acknowledged the breach, took responsibility, and announced both the CBI probe and a shift to computer-based testing from 2027, specifically to close the loophole. A little over a month later, the re-exam was actually conducted nationwide. Whether that response was sufficient is a fair debate — but "nothing happened" simply isn't true.

It's also worth remembering this wasn't the first attempt at reform. A committee had already been constituted after earlier exam failures to fix exactly this problem, and the minister's own statement was that the system had failed again despite it. That's a more honest admission than most public officials make. And it raises the uncomfortable but necessary question: even if the Education Minister is replaced tomorrow, if someone lower in the chain decides to leak a paper, does the outcome actually change? Structural fixes — moving the exam online, removing the human point of failure — arguably do more to prevent a repeat than a resignation would.

None of this requires anyone to defend the minister personally, and no one should feel obligated to preface every comment with a disclaimer of "I don't support him" just to be allowed to state a fact. Nobody imagines he's some towering figure people are dying to protect. But the reverse is equally true: stating what actually happened, in order, isn't the same as taking his side. Assuming that anyone who complicates the narrative must be defending him is exactly the kind of lazy binary thinking that keeps a real conversation from happening.

That's really the core problem — false binaries. You don't have to support Sonam Wangchuk to think this issue matters. You don't have to support a CJP-led protest, or stand with any single group leading it, to still believe the Education Minister has failed and should be held accountable. You can refuse to share a platform with people who mock what you hold sacred, or who've excused actual terrorism, and still demand better from the government. Demanding accountability doesn't require surrendering your principles, endorsing someone else's politics, or joining every protest placed in front of you. There's a real difference between independent thinking and blind camp politics — and most of what's circulating online collapses that difference on purpose.

Part of why it collapses so easily is the feed itself. Your timeline isn't showing you the whole protest — it's showing you the version you're most likely to engage with. If you support the protest, you'll be served police crackdowns and tear gas. If you oppose it, you'll be served stone-pelting and attacks on police. The algorithm doesn't care what actually happened; it only cares whether you keep watching. Before forming an opinion, it's worth remembering your feed was never impartial to begin with — the fix isn't forced neutrality, it's actively seeking out both sides, checking multiple credible sources, and looking at the full timeline instead of a single clip.

It's also worth asking why this particular pattern of protest feels so familiar — predictable, almost. Long before this movement took shape, there was already commentary anticipating a wave of protests along near-identical lines in the years ahead: NEET-related unrest, doctors' protests, farmer agitations, and others, each eventually folding into broader anti-government messaging. That doesn't make the underlying grievances fake. But it should make you curious enough to actually look into the history and affiliations of the student organisations fronting these movements, rather than assuming every banner represents an unaffiliated, spontaneous voice of students.

Two people can hold completely different opinions about the government and still be respectful to each other. If political inclination is spoiling your relationships with friends, colleagues, or family, it isn't worth it. The party trying to pull the government down was in power for decades and, by most accounts, didn't care much about ordinary citizens either. The party in power now has held that position for over a decade and shows many of the same instincts. Both, ultimately, seem more interested in staying in power and shaping the narrative than in the people they claim to represent. So don't let either side turn you into a foot soldier for their fight.

Is it possible that the misguided youth of our country are romanticizing the idea of protest — standing up for a cause, loving the vibe, enjoying the display of perceived humanity while ticking it off some invisible bucket list? The domino effect, the sheep mentality, all playing out together in one big soup. Ordinarily I'd have no problem watching that unfold. But the truth is, the ultimate beneficiary of full-blown political turmoil isn't just a weak, opportunistic opposition — it's the foreign forces we all know are waiting for exactly this kind of instability. The same forces behind what happened in Bangladesh, in Nepal. Any half-decent political analyst saw this coming; this is no longer about one party or petty politics, it is now directly playing into the hands of foreign interference. Divide and rule — straight out of the British playbook. It's no coincidence that protests and hunger strikes were non-productive tools when we were colonized: the British loved them precisely because they could always be managed and contained — sadly, not unlike what our own forces have at times been ordered to do since.

A productive protest looks like a shoe factory worker going on strike by only making the left shoe — so that once demands are met, they make the right one too, and nobody actually loses anything. What's happening here is different. They asked for accountability and they got action. What they didn't get was everything — meaning Dharmendra Pradhan's resignation — but that was always the least realistic demand on the list.

Yes, compensation should absolutely go to the families of students who took their own lives in the aftermath of this. May God be with them. As a state, we failed them — I won't deny that. But rallying behind a cause fronted by SFI, AISA, KYS, AISF — is that really where we've landed? Standing anywhere near people like Arundhati Roy, or fans of Umar Khalid, who've raised anti-Hindu, anti-India slogans or defended others who did — is that who we're lending our voice to now? How far down are we willing to go out of sheer desperation?

Even Ambedkar warned us about this back in 1949, when he told the Constitutional Assembly that if every group turned to fasts and satyagraha to press its demands, it would breed anarchy, hollow out democratic institutions, and risk sliding toward dictatorship — even if the public stayed fully informed along the way. His point wasn't that dissent should be silenced; it was that a democracy survives on constitutional morality, not on emotional or theatrical methods of protest. He believed the right way to pursue change was to advocate, educate, agitate, and organize — but through political and constitutional channels, not by holding the state hostage. That distinction is exactly what's missing from this conversation.

Bottom line: understand the difference between a protest that aims for accountability and a protest that aims for destabilization. One shouldn't be protesting just to stay relevant, for better reach, or because everyone else is talking about it. God help us, because with this level of collective unwillingness to ask basic questions, we're going to need it.

An informed citizen will do his research, not fall for trends.

Yours faithfully, Khushansh B
